**Modern post-training has become a brute-force paradigm of curating ever more environments and ever longer training horizons.** In large part, this is because frontier Transformers are still poor at *compositional generalization*, the ability to solve unseen problems by composing familiar ones. Unless our models compose the individual lessons they learn, every new domain will demand its own investment in the form of training data.

Training data is not the only lever, of course. For the past few years, we've attacked harder tasks by *scaffolding* the Transformer, first with chain-of-thought reasoning, then with tool use, and so on. Yet across all of this, *generalization* itself has been left to the underlying neural network and to its 2017 token-level inductive biases. **We argue that better generalization is largely the job of what today is called a *harness*.** A harness is the program that sits between the external world and the neural network: it decides how to encode the current state of the environment, which can be arbitrarily long and complex, into one or more inputs to the LLM and how to determine the next action. **The primary job of the harness should be to carry a higher-level inductive bias that can reduce unfamiliar and complex problems to compositions of simpler ones for the underlying neural network.**

Concretely, we think a good harness is one that **shapes each call to the underlying Transformer so that every observation is *locally in-distribution***, i.e. each Transformer call handles a prompt that is in-distribution with respect to its training data. **In fact, a good harness can frequently reduce problems that seem to require breakthroughs in post-training into almost mundane capabilities of the existing generation of language models.** We first showed a version of this for long context processing [[nearly a year ago](https://alexzhang13.github.io/blog/2025/rlm/)] and, in this post, we show that this principle extends to the efficiency of *learning* itself. That is, **what a model learns through a well-designed harness generalizes across task lengths and across domains** far better than training the neural network on its own does.

**We test this by using reinforcement learning (RL) to train a Recursive Language Model (RLM)**, a harness in which the model offloads its context and defers execution to programmatic decomposition and recursive sub-calls. **In our experiments below, training on only short tasks generalizes to held-out tasks 8–32x longer, with roughly 10x the eval lift with the same train lift over training the underlying Transformer directly.** Moreover, training on one domain transfers to other domains at a far better rate than that of a vanilla Transformer. We observe this generalization effect because **the RLM harness induces an equivalence relation between tasks with latent similarities**, meaning the RLM's main context sees nearly the *same* token-level trajectory between these tasks. These results suggest that similar to tuning model architectures and training recipes, **well-designed harnesses can reduce the cost of curating more data and generating longer rollouts while increasing the coverage of tasks solvable through post-training**.

## Compositional generalization is the bare minimum.

Many open problems in AI reduce down to the issue that training deep neural networks (DNNs) on naturally occurring text data does not always generalize to novel tasks. In fact, this is the issue that post-training efforts attempt to bandage with targeted environments based on real-world benchmarks and usage. However, the tasks we want to apply language models to have become increasingly long and expensive (e.g. long-horizon), making the brute-force strategy of training on everything more difficult.

Instead, we argue that a necessary and scalable property of AI systems is **compositional generalization**, which is the ability to solve unseen problems by composing familiar concepts and patterns. Compositional generalization enables the reachable task space of your system to extend beyond your training set's coverage (including tasks that do not *fit* in an LM's context window), especially for tasks that may look nothing alike in their surface-level tokens while sharing the same structure.

To further motivate compositional generalization from the perspective of the *nature* of tasks in the wild, the [**Mismanaged Geniuses Hypothesis (MGH)**](https://alexzhang13.github.io/blog/2026/mgh/) <d-cite key="zhang2026mgh"></d-cite> attempts to characterize tasks humans care about solving, arguing that most difficult tasks of interest can be decomposed naturally into sub-tasks that existing LMs can solve.

The intuition is that humans have proposed and solved some of these tasks, and humans have a bounded working memory, so the tasks we tend to solve are composed from human collaboration. Directly training on these tasks is extremely hard and expensive, so this hypothesis would imply that compositional generalization enables solving open problems by training on simpler problems. We can't provably confirm this hypothesis to be true, but it provides a starting point for why compositional generalization may be necessary.

### Transformers are unreliable at compositional generalization.

Language model capabilities have this strange property of both solving incredibly difficult tasks, while also failing at comparable or easier tasks, at least per human intuition. In other words, LM capabilities are not guaranteed to be *transitive*, so while a human can likely learn to solve a set of tasks $\mathcal{X}$ after solving a task $x$, LMs often cannot.

There is nothing logical preventing a deep neural network (e.g. Transformer) from internally decomposing a complex input into familiar pieces at a neural level. For example, convolutional networks impose a positional invariant to allow models to learn local patterns over a global task. Prior works in interpretability have attempted to study generalization properties of training Transformers at a model architecture-level, citing various circuits / mechanisms <d-cite key="olsson2022induction,garg2022icl,vonoswald2022transformers"></d-cite>, symmetries <d-cite key="nanda2023grokking"></d-cite>, and toy settings <d-cite key="chughtai2023toy"></d-cite> where task generalization is observed. The conclusion is that generalization in training Transformers is rarely guaranteed, even if the underlying learned behavior is something seen during training <d-cite key="wang2024ood"></d-cite>. With this observation in mind, a natural question is, for an arbitrary task, whether we can shape the input prompt(s) $P$ given to the Transformer-based language model to provide better generalization guarantees.

## Compositional generalization comes from the harness, not a base model.

In modern applications, language models (LMs) are typically deployed as an *agent*, which receive some state $s$ and perform an action $a$ to interact with an environment $E$. The most naive form of an agent is the base Transformer acting as a language model, but when the state and action space are arbitrarily large and complex, we typically introduce a *harness* $H: s \rightarrow a$ that defines a program over language model call(s) and external tools to serve as the agent.

Traditionally, we think of harnesses like Claude Code or Codex as necessary because of their ability to invoke external tools inside a complicated environment. But an additional benefit *independent of interacting with the environment* is the ability to simplify the arbitrarily complex state $s$ into potentially many smaller observations $o$ that each individual LM call in the harness can properly handle. We know that Transformers are unreliable at compositional generalization, so we can rely on the harness instead.

A good harness is a harness that *reduces unfamiliar problems to familiar ones* and *reduces complex problems to simple ones*. In other words, even if the state $s$ is out-of-distribution (OOD) to what any individual language model call was trained for, a good harness produces observations $o$ that are ***locally in-distribution (LID)***, which we define as every individual LM call over this observation being in-distribution with respect to the training data.

### Good harnesses induce equivalence classes over tasks.

A major pitfall of existing harness designs such as Claude Code and Codex that prevents observations from being locally in-distribution (LID) is the behavior of the *main LM / main context*, which is flooded with interleaved task-specific information, tool call outputs, and reasoning that get continuously appended. This design is highly useful because all information coming in acts as a growing history that the LM in theory has a full view on. However, these bloated histories quickly become OOD for the LLM ingesting the main context, manifesting in the "[context rot](https://www.trychroma.com/research/context-rot)" phenomenon that we often observe in practice where the model becomes unreasonably "dumb" after a long-running agent session<d-footnote>This is not unique to LLMs. Humans also get similarly distracted if they are forced to distribute their attention simultaneously across many things at once.</d-footnote>.

The current solution is to rely on curating more environments and training on billions or trillions of agent traces, which has proven to be a slow and extremely expensive process to make progress on<d-footnote>This is evidenced by the large market for RL environment and data companies.</d-footnote>. One may argue that, through enough post-training, frontier models will eventually see all such practical behavior, but currently it's not clear. An optimist may go further and argue that the internal architectural mechanisms of the language model will *grok / generalize* this kind of behavior over time, but so far this has not proven to be true.

The core issue is that despite many workflows being similar or composable across trajectories, they often *appear* as unique tasks to the main context of these harnesses. Instead, we propose that a good harness should **enable individual LM calls to see structurally similar tasks as isomorphic**, enabling compositional generalization. Formally, a harness induces an equivalence operator $\sim_{H}$ over the set of all task states $\mathcal{T}$, meaning structurally similar tasks fall under the same harness-induced equivalence class and produce the same or a similar observation $o$ to the LM call. For most harnesses that use a main LM with sub-agents, it is generally sufficient to view this argument with respect to the main context, assuming the sub-agents deal with small in-distribution sub-tasks, yielding a locally in-distribution observation.

<div style="margin: 1.5em 0">
$$
\text{For any }\tau',\ \{\tau \in \mathcal{T} : \tau' \sim_{H} \tau\}\text{ are viewed as the same observation (within some ball }\epsilon\text{) in the harness }H.
$$
</div>

Harnesses reduce the space of learnable task states $\mathcal{T}$ to a **harness-induced quotient set (Hi/Q set) of trajectories $\mathcal{T} / \sim_{H}$**, meaning a proper harness only has to learn from one task in each equivalence class to generalize to tasks within that equivalence class. A well-designed harness not only reduces the space of learnable trajectories to a small $\mathcal{T} / \sim_{H}$, but it also generalizes to a wider class of trajectories than the set of available training tasks<d-footnote>It is important to note that "harness" here can mean both the program and the LM itself, depending on the design of the harness. Harnesses like the RLM depend on the LM to make decisions, and therefore the Hi/Q sets are induced by the harness and model together. Training a model can therefore change these equivalence classes, as we will see.</d-footnote>, including those that do not fit in the base model's context window.

## RLMs are surprisingly good at compositional generalization.

### Equivalence classes over trajectories induced by the RLM harness.

An ideal harness decomposes a task such that the main context is abstracted to **literally** appear token-for-token similar for similar problems, i.e. those that fall under the same harness-induced equivalence class $[\tau'] = \{\tau \in \mathcal{T} : \tau' \sim \tau\}$. This isomorphism enables generalizations of the form: *if a system can solve task X, it should transitively be able to solve task Y*.

<figure>
<center>
    <img src="{{ '/assets/img/lm_compo/fig4_context_offloading_programmatic_subcalls.png' | relative_url }}" style="width:100%; margin-bottom: 10px" alt="Figure 4: Context offloading and programmatic sub-agent calling">
</center>
    <figcaption style="width:100%; margin:auto"><strong>Figure 4.</strong> We compare how context offloading and programmatic sub-agent calling, two primary components of the RLM, enable the root LM to view two different problems as the same / similar. <strong>(1)</strong> Standard agent methods stuff the entire query and context as a prefix to the prompt. Between two different tasks, this prefix significantly changes the output distribution of the model, even if the tasks are solved the same way. Context offloading allows similar problems to be viewed as the same. <strong>(2)</strong> Standard agent methods use tool calls, which return information directly to the main context. Programmatic sub-agent calling allows intermediate computations and information to be stored in the REPL, so the main context does not need to see task-specific information, which can alter its output distribution.</figcaption>
</figure>

The Recursive Language Model (RLM) harness is designed around abstracting tasks by their decomposition and deferring task-specific information to sub-calls. In this sense, RLMs view similarly decomposed problems as isomorphic, and form the **Hi/Q set** in this way<d-footnote>Notably, the argument below holds recursively, as we can treat each offloaded sub-agent as its own instance with its own “main” context. This is the intended design of an RLM being recursive, but it is not strictly necessary for maintaining locally in-distribution observations.</d-footnote>. In Figure 4 and below, we justify the design choices around the RLM for inducing a tighter **Hi/Q**.

**(1) Context offloading**, where task-specific context is offloaded somewhere so the root LM does not directly see it, enables two different problems to appear similar at the first step. Recently, several scaffolds have adopted this feature as a form of context management (see [Anthropic's Managed Agents blog](https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/managed-agents)), but we argue it alone is not sufficient for compositional generalization.

On its own, context offloading does not prevent environment feedback and/or sub-agent information from returning back to the main context. Over long trajectory horizons, the main context becomes OOD, breaking LID.

**(2) Programmatic tool / sub-agent calling**, where tools and sub-agents are treated as functions in a code REPL, allow the root LM to selectively choose information and pass around information across further tool calls and sub-agents without the root LM itself ever needing to see it. This includes tool and sub-call outputs, which can be stored in variables in memory directly and accessed by future sub-calls. Programmatic sub-calling is equally as important as context offloading for abstracting task-specific information away from the main context.

### RLMs can train on short tasks and generalize to longer, unseen tasks.

One of our key findings about Transformer-based LMs is that training on a domain at a particular context length does not generalize well to longer context lengths <d-cite key="anil2022length"></d-cite>. A large chunk of mid-training and post-training work in production models like [Qwen 3.x](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.09388), [Kimi K2.x](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.20534), [GLM 5.x](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.15763), etc. has been dedicated to carefully introducing longer and longer data into the training mix so LMs can learn to handle those context lengths, but many modern applications commonly exceed those lengths. The length generalization issue especially impacts standard agent designs like [ReAct](https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.03629) <d-cite key="yao2023react"></d-cite>, [CodeAct](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.01030) <d-cite key="wang2024codeact"></d-cite>, [Claude Code](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/overview), [Codex](https://openai.com/codex/), etc. which as we mentioned, rely on appending observations to a growing prefix history of context.

We hypothesize that for RLMs, similar tasks of a different length can fall under the same equivalence class under $\sim_{RLM}$. In the following experiment, we explore how training only the root LM of an RLM exclusively on short sequences can generalize sequences an order of magnitude longer, as we posit that the decomposition, and hence the trajectory the RLM's root LM sees on both settings, are nearly identical.

We consider a suite of 6 different environments across different axes of length: varying the input length, output length, and number of instructions. We explicitly train only on a split containing short tasks, and evaluate on a split only containing significantly longer tasks within the same domain. Across all the settings below, we train for 150 steps (batch size 64, 4 rollouts per) with `prime-rl` ([Decoupled PPO](https://verl.readthedocs.io/en/latest/algo/rollout_corr_math.html#decoupled-ppo-achieving-batch-size-invariance) <d-cite key="hilton2022batch"></d-cite> with GRPO-like advantages + KL loss) and evaluate every 10 steps.

- [**MRCRv2**](https://github.com/google-deepmind/eval_hub/tree/master/eval_hub/mrcr_v2) (64k, 2-needle setting to 2M, 8-needle setting). A commonly reported benchmark in frontier model reports, the premise is to find the $i$th instance of needle sentence that answers some query within a large corpus of conversation data.
- [**GraphWalks**](https://huggingface.co/datasets/openai/graphwalks) (<128K to >1M setting). Another commonly reported benchmark in frontier model reports, the premise is to extract nodes from a graph that satisfy some simple constraint.
- [**LongBenchPro**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.02872) (32k to 256k setting, English-only). A suite of multiple choice-style questions spanning 11 different QA, code, and reasoning style problems.
- [**OOLONG**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.02817) [trec-coarse] (32k to 256k setting). A set of aggregation style questions to answer statistics about a dataset of information.
- [**OOLONG-Pairs**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.24601) (8k to 32k input, 7k to 146k output setting). A modified variant to find pairs of elements in the OOLONG dataset satisfying certain constraints.
- [**Ada-LEval**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.06480) [best-answer] (8k to 128k setting). Given a question and a large number of potential candidate and distractor answers, pick out the most suitable to answer the question.

We plot both the training reward on short tasks (translucent) and the evaluation reward (dark) on the longer version of the tasks. We compare training an RLM, an RLM where we nudge it to state the decomposition it wants to perform, and a base Transformer (with YaRN for longer settings). We use [**Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507**](https://huggingface.co/Qwen/Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507) as the model for all settings.

Across all six tasks, the RLM yields significantly better eval results on long tasks by training exclusively on short tasks, beating out the base Transformer by a significant margin even when starting with a lower step-0 eval performance. On MRCRv2, GraphWalks, OOLONG, and OOLONG-Pairs, the trained Qwen3-30B-A3B-Instruct-2507 RLM approaches or exceeds an RLM with a frontier model, GPT-5.5 on the long eval, while far exceeding the base Transformer.

**RLM eval performance on longer tasks more closely matches the train reward on shorter tasks.** On all six tasks, the RLM yields significant improvements in performance on the long eval relative to its starting, step-0 eval performance. The base Transformer generally yields flat eval performance despite the train reward growing and often exceeding the train reward of the RLM, suggesting *what is learned by the Transformer does not extrapolate to the longer setting*.

**Why length generalization occurs and where it fails.** In all of the environments above, the strategy the RLM learns to solve the short tasks is roughly equivalent to that of the strategy needed to solve the longer tasks, and because the root LM's view of the long task with the context offloaded looks near token-for-token identical to the short task, the RLM has effectively seen the same task during training. In other words, many tasks share the same equivalence class across length. The primary difference is the number of sub-calls and the content of the sub-calls themselves, each of which handle an in-distribution sub-task. Length generalization occurs because the RLM learns a generalizable strategy, *but this is not always guaranteed*. In many of the short settings above, one viable strategy is to just offload the entire problem to a single sub-call and return this as a solution, effectively becoming equivalent to the long-context Transformer baseline.

We include the "nudge to decompose" user message variant (a condensed version [of the addendum in the RLM repository](https://github.com/alexzhang13/rlm/blob/main/rlm/utils/prompts.py#L147)) to illustrate this point, which helps in the case of MRCRv2, where the RLM does not learn the right, generalizable strategy across length. In the training of RLMs and locally in-distribution LM systems as a whole, a whole area of study is how much supervision / distillation is required to get the system to converge to a generalizable solution over a non-generalizable solution. Our intuition is that at scale, no supervision is necessary, but for the sake of sample efficient learning, [some form of supervision or hint is helpful](https://noahziems.com/pedagogical-rl)<d-footnote>One may wonder whether, with infinite scale (e.g. you can train on all possible 32k length sequences), generalization across say length will naturally emerge inside of a DNN architecture. I’d guess the answer to be no, and that composition does not emerge so easily, but one could also easily argue that training locally in-distribution harnesses is far more sample efficient. An interesting direction is therefore, to uncover differences in how these systems scale with respect to data and environments.</d-footnote>.

### RLMs can generalize to unseen tasks that share latent structure observed in training.

The previous section showed that RLMs can learn to view tasks and their length-extrapolated variants as isomorphic by abstracting them as the same task in the root model. For length extrapolation in particular, if the RLM learns a length-agnostic strategy, it quickly generalizes to longer lengths. We can take this intuition to generalization on tasks with shared decompositions: if the RLM learns a strategy that generalizes from one domain to another (e.g. applying sorting, filter and search, MapReduce, etc.) it can similarly generalize by abstracting away the domain-specific tokens, and viewing the two problems as equivalent.

We consider 3 domains in which the context and query are referring to completely different domains, but the actual underlying strategy is roughly the same.

- **OOLONG (trec-coarse questions → spam questions).** Training on aggregation tasks about Jeopardy TREC questions and evaluating on aggregation tasks about questions whose answer is either SPAM or HAM.
- **OBLIQ-Bench Analogues (writing → math).** Training on tasks about searching for essays that seem to be written by the same author, and evaluating on tasks about searching for math problems that require the same reasoning process. Train and eval metric are nDCG@10.
- **OBLIQ-Bench Descriptive (Twitter stance → Wildchat errors).** Training on tasks about finding tweets that satisfy a certain stance, and evaluating on tasks about searching for Wildchat conversations containing errors. Train and eval metric are nDCG@10.

We similarly find that the RLM exhibits clear generalization capabilities on **completely different domains** than the one it trained on, while the base Transformer struggles to meaningfully improve. Similar to in the length generalization experiments, initial improvements in eval performance on OBLIQ-Bench during the base Transformer training generally come from learning to follow the proper answer format, which quickly diminishes. Interestingly, *the train reward of the base Transformer generally exceeds that of the RLM despite clear gaps in performance on the eval*. Instead, the train reward of the RLM closely matches the trend of the eval reward, despite being on a completely different domain.

Even more than the length generalization, these experiments have completely different token distributions between train and test, as the main similarity is in the latent task structure that the model must learn to exploit. Like in length generalization, these results suggest the internal mechanisms of the Transformer struggle to decompose the tasks into composable patterns that generalize.

**On the cost of training harnesses like RLMs.** On similarly-sized tasks, the RLM incurs an extra runtime and memory cost over naively training a Transformer. In particular, across both the length and strategy generalization experiments, the runtime of RLM training is 1.5-3x longer than the base Transformer counterparts due to multiple steps per sample and waiting on sub-calls. However, this cost scales well with task complexity, as training a Transformer on longer context / horizon tasks is significantly more expensive. Even training a simple ReAct agent on the tasks above on an 8xH100 is difficult for a 30B model due to context bloat.

Compositional generalization is important because for each element in the **Hi/Q set**, a harness only needs to train on the "easiest" sample to generalize to the others. For the RLM in particular, this is purely a function of how compactly a sequence of LM programs can express the task solution.

### How close are unseen RLM task trajectories to train trajectories?

For a language model $\theta$ and a task prompt $x$, we care about its output distribution

<div>
$$
P_x(y_{1:T}) = \prod_{t=1}^{T} p_{\theta}(y_t \mid x, y_{\lt t}).
$$
</div>

When we say a task is "in-distribution", we generally mean that the model yields a reasonable answer conditioned on the prompt $x$.

It is rather difficult to characterize whether a prompt $x$ is "in-distribution", but we can use benchmark performance as a proxy for this kind of thing — e.g., LMs can solve most AIME problems, so we consider these tasks to be "in-distribution". For agent trajectories, which are often long, multiple turns, and filled with extra fluff like tool calls and harness tokens, it is even harder to determine whether two trajectories will exhibit the same output distribution behavior.

If the RLM produces a trajectory on an unseen task that is *token-for-token* identical to a trajectory generated during a training task, then we could easily argue that these two tasks are in the same equivalence class under $\sim_{RLM}$ and are viewed as isomorphic. Unfortunately, even small, one-token perturbations can semantically change the meaning of a text sequence, so arguing that $P_x$ and $P_{x'}$ have some bounded difference is difficult. In the **Appendix**, we discuss some proxies for determining whether two trajectories fall under the same equivalence class. In many cases, the RLM will still choose to print out task-specific information and pass it back to the main context. ### The Scaling Picture and Moving Forward

Personally I think the results above are sort of beautiful in a scientific way. But fundamentally the question is how this fits into the scaling picture and the existing model / harness landscape<d-footnote>As a note here, one downside of introducing new scaffolds / harnesses without explicit support from a large model is that even if your harness is designed very well, if the model is not trained to use it, it will pay a large performance cost. For RLMs in particular, the idea is that a frontier model can be really good at “reasoning” through a problem with some sequence of reasoning tokens, but if it equivalently wrote out the same reasoning process through programmatic tool / sub-agent calling, it actually will yield degraded reasoning as they are different token distributions! One interesting example is that RLMs on Opus 4.8 / Fable produce noticeably better trajectories than GPT-5.5, Gemini 3.5, etc. which likely is related to the introduction of Dynamic Workflows in Claude Code and training around this capability.</d-footnote>.

One might be tempted to conclude that we should impose our intuition about well-defined programmatic strategies such as MapReduce, sorting, dynamic programming, etc. and distill these as equivalence classes into the training of our new systems. While this may be a viable training strategy for warming up the model to learn how to use the harness, it misses the core benefit of training locally in-distribution systems. Many problems in the wild likely share some kind of latent structure, which may not be easy to define or as clear as in the benchmarks we see above. We showed in our experiments that generalizable strategies and the **Hi/Q** set are induced by the harness and naturally learned through naive RL, rather than distilled. Similar to how generalization naturally emerged from RL training RLMs, a more favorable approach would be to naively scale around these harnesses with the same post-training recipes and environments we already use<d-footnote>An interesting scientific question is when you want to train in a locally in-distribution way, and when you want to focus on the base capabilities of the model. This could be as simple as the separation between pre-training and post-training, but I suspect it might be more complicated than that.</d-footnote>.

In the end, we care about more capable and expressive ways of scaling and using language models, and what a proper harness provides is 1) the ability to extend the capabilities of a base LM to novel tasks; 2) the ability to efficiently train around the harness to yield stronger, more generalizable systems. In some sense, we are arguing that harness design is effectively a part of model architecture design, and that compositional generalization is largely unlocked at the level of the harness. We focus this blog on RLMs, which provide an example of one class of these systems, but we independently motivate harnesses that provide similar guarantees around what the models learn. The perspective of task equivalence classes and the **Hi/Q set** over trajectories are a way to understand how harnesses shape their observations to be locally in-distribution.

At its core, this blog is about an extremely simple but important idea in harness design: make sure these systems act in a way such that each language model call is dealing with something we know it can solve.

### Appendix

Recall that we are interested in characterizing how close harness trajectories on unseen tasks are to what they learned during training. It is rare for two LM trajectories to be token-for-token identical, but taking some liberties with rough smoothness assumptions on the LM as a function of its input, what we really care about is whether two trajectories are close enough under some metric so their output distributions are almost the same.

It is very hard to formalize the above in an empirically meaningful way; we would need to know some properties about the function defined by the LM, a distance metric on input prompts that are within some $\delta$-ball, and a distance metric on the output logits that are within some $\epsilon$-ball. As a basic start, we are interested in, for some distance metric $d(\cdot,\cdot)$, the difference between the root LM trajectory of the RLM during evaluation and training. We can define "isomorphic" here loosely as two prompts have some distance less than $\epsilon$, but as mentioned earlier a more rigorous treatment of this topic is likely necessary. We generally just want to understand and find, for this distance metric, what is the average distance of the *closest* training pair to the evaluation rollouts? In other words, at training step $t$, for trajectories $x$, the set of evaluation samples $\mathcal{E}_t$, and the set of training samples $\mathcal{T}_{\leq t}$ that occurred before, we compute (with a bit of abuse of notation):

<div>
$$
\mathbb{E}_{e \sim \mathcal{E}_t} \left[\min_{\tau \in \mathcal{T}_{\leq t}} d(x_{\tau},x_{e}) \right] \approx \frac{1}{|\mathcal{E}_t|} \sum_{e \in \mathcal{E}_t}\min_{\tau \in \mathcal{T}_{\leq t}} d(x_{\tau},x_{e})
$$
</div>

We plot the similarity, i.e. $1 - d(\cdot,\cdot)$, of the average eval rollout of the best RLM vs. base Transformer checkpoint to any training trajectory that occurred before it. We consider the following distance metrics:

| Plot label | Metric | Distance definition $d(x_{\text{train}}, x_{\text{eval}})$ |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Edit** | Token-level, length-normalized Levenshtein Distance | Let $\operatorname{Lev}(\cdot,\cdot)$ be token-level Levenshtein distance and define normalized similarity $\operatorname{LevSim}(a,b)=1-\dfrac{\operatorname{Lev}(a,b)}{\max(\lVert a\rVert,\lVert b\rVert)}$. Then $d(a,b)=1-\operatorname{LevSim}(a,b)=\dfrac{\operatorname{Lev}(a,b)}{\max(\lVert a\rVert,\lVert b\rVert)}.$ |
| **Contain** | 3-gram word containment | Let $N_3(x)$ be the multiset of word 3-grams in $x$. Then (asymmetric): $d(x_{\text{train}},x_{\text{eval}})=1-\frac{\lvert N_3(x_{\text{eval}})\cap N_3(x_{\text{train}})\rvert}{\lvert N_3(x_{\text{eval}})\rvert}.$ |
| **Jaccard** | 3-gram Jaccard | $d(x_{\text{train}},x_{\text{eval}})=1-\frac{\lvert N_3(x_{\text{eval}})\cap N_3(x_{\text{train}})\rvert}{\lvert N_3(x_{\text{eval}})\cup N_3(x_{\text{train}})\rvert}.$ |
| **Weighted Jaccard** | Weighted Jaccard | Let $c_x(t)$ be the token (or n-gram) count of type $t$ in $x$. Then $d(x_{\text{train}},x_{\text{eval}})=1-\frac{\sum_t \min\!\big(c_{x_{\text{train}}}(t),c_{x_{\text{eval}}}(t)\big)}{\sum_t \max\!\big(c_{x_{\text{train}}}(t),c_{x_{\text{eval}}}(t)\big)}.$ |
| **Length** | Length Ratio | (Content-blind) with sequence length $\lVert x\rVert$: $d(x_{\text{train}},x_{\text{eval}})=1-\frac{\min(\lVert x_{\text{train}}\rVert,\lVert x_{\text{eval}}\rVert)}{\max(\lVert x_{\text{train}}\rVert,\lVert x_{\text{eval}}\rVert)}.$ |
